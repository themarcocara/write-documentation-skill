**1. Vocabulary**

P2 deploys a layered register that mixes technical precision with evaluative, colloquial, and first-person language. P1 uses only functional vocabulary — terms that describe what a system does, with no evaluative or personal register.

- Write in first person throughout all technical documents, including specifications and reference material. Own recommendations, considerations, and conclusions explicitly with "I propose," "I believe," "I considered," "I plan," "I would like" — not passive constructions or institution-voice.
- When introducing technical terms or product categories, contextualise them with evaluative language: not just what something is, but why it matters, how it ranks, what it costs you. Make evaluation visible within the definition.
- Surface uncertainty with hedged qualifiers rather than suppressing it: "It is unknown whether," "I'm not convinced that," "this may not be entirely accurate," "possibly," "likely." Treat acknowledged uncertainty as part of the argument, not a weakness.
- Use informal and colloquial expressions inside technical sentences rather than segregating them to casual passages. Casual verb phrases from everyday speech, conversational shorthand, and light humour belong within sentences that otherwise carry technical content.
- Use subjective evaluative adjectives in technical contexts: "compelling," "superior," "modest," "heavy." These are judgments, not descriptions; deploy them as such.
- When dismissing an option, state the dismissal in personal-stakes language: "I ruled out X primarily because of Y" rather than "X does not satisfy requirement Y."
- Use parenthetical asides inside sentences to add reasoning, caveats, or self-directed meta-commentary. The aside should add something the main clause left out — a cost, a contingency, a correction, a clarification the writer noticed mid-sentence.
- Use Latin abbreviations and informal shorthand as unmarked vocabulary, not as formal signals.

**2. Sentence Structure**

P2 uses radical variation in sentence length, tolerates fragments as complete answers, and trails sentences into open contingency rather than closing them definitively. P1 maintains consistent medium-length sentences with clean, declarative closings and almost no structural variation.

- When a list item's answer is genuinely simple, make it a fragment: one word or one short phrase stands alone as the complete answer. Do not expand it to achieve visual symmetry with other list items.
- Begin sentences with coordinating conjunctions to continue reasoning across sentence boundaries without reformulating the entire argument into a single complex clause.
- Follow a long multi-clause sentence with a short declarative one that names the consequence or the conclusion. Vary sentence length within paragraphs intentionally rather than settling at a consistent middle register.
- Use parenthetical asides that interrupt the main sentence to insert self-referential commentary — what was missed, what might change, what the writer would add if there were more time. These belong mid-argument, not in footnotes.
- After a recommendation, add a sentence that names the exact condition under which the recommendation should be revisited. The condition is specific and testable, not vague. End the section there rather than restating the recommendation.
- Mix numbered instructions with explanatory prose: some steps are introduced by a paragraph that explains the reasoning; the command or parameter follows. Do not keep all procedural content in a discrete, uniform numbered list format.
- When ending a section, trail into possibility or contingency rather than closing with a summary. Leave the reader at an open question, a future action item, or a next decision to be made.

**3. Rhetorical Techniques**

P2 shows the deliberation behind decisions rather than only the decisions themselves. It uses concession-assertion patterns, explicit uncertainty, comparative data, and social attribution to build arguments. P1 states results without deliberation and presents structure as taxonomy rather than argument.

- Structure recommendation sections by narrating the consideration process: name all options evaluated, rank them with reasons, name the winner with its trade-offs stated explicitly. Open with the problem being decided, not the conclusion.
- When presenting data that contradicts an expectation, use the concession-assertion move: state the expectation first, then name the data that contradicts it. Do not omit the expectation just because the data overrides it.
- Name what you do not know, and name what you would need to observe before making that decision. Frame knowledge gaps as open items in the reasoning chain, not as omissions to apologise for.
- When comparing options, give each option data in the same format so comparisons are direct. For each option, name both the advantage and the cost — even for the option you are recommending against.
- Connect technical decisions to user or player outcomes explicitly, stating the link in the sentence rather than leaving it implicit. A technical decision should resolve to a legible human benefit within the same paragraph.
- When attributing a design idea, a code contribution, or a collaborative decision, name who was involved and what their role was. Social attribution is part of how arguments are built, not merely an acknowledgment.
- Add retrospective self-commentary within or after sections: what was missed, what might have been approached differently, what would require revisiting. This is argumentative, not apologetic.
- Frame future milestones and plans as contingent on earlier outcomes, using conditional constructions. Avoid stating future plans as commitments when they are actually conditional on data not yet collected.

**4. Voice Qualities**

P2's voice is specifically personal: it expresses enthusiasm, doubt, ownership, and collaborative awareness in a way that distinguishes an individual from an institutional source. P1's voice is institutional and generic — competent, but without a person behind it.

- Address a specific, known reader rather than a generic audience. Write as if the document is being sent to a named collaborator, not published for an anonymous public. What to explain and what to leave implicit should reflect that relationship.
- Maintain a consistent distinction between the first-person singular for personal judgments and the first-person plural for shared team decisions. Personal recommendations belong in "I"; decisions about team direction or shared resources belong in "we."
- Express enthusiasm narrowly and contextually: use it where the stakes of the idea genuinely warrant it, and only once per section at most. The enthusiasm should be earned by what preceded it.
- Name gaps in your own document explicitly and without apology: identify what was not considered, what might be added later, what information may be inaccurate. This increases credibility rather than undermining it.
- State beliefs as beliefs, not as impersonal observations. Own the assertion, then flag its contingency. Do not use distancing constructions that separate the writer from the claim.
- Let the writer's personal stakes appear in technical decisions: name what something would cost the team, what the writer is willing to invest, what the writer wants to try. The document should carry evidence of investment in the outcome.
- Use retrospective structure: explain why decisions were made, not just what was decided. The reasoning behind a past decision is part of the argument, not background colour.
- When a question has a genuinely short answer, give the short answer and stop. Do not elaborate for the sake of proportion or to match the length of adjacent sections. Restraint at the right moment is as much a voice quality as elaboration.
